Japan 2025

9/30 - 10/14

Our plan this trip was to explore Hokkaido. We will fly into Tokyo and stay a few nights, then fly into Hakodate, rent a car and drive north to Niseko (day trip to Lake Toya), then drive to Sapporo along the coast (stop by Cape Kamui and Otaru), and finally fly back to Tokyo for a few days before flying home.

10/5 - 10/6

Niseko

From Hakodate, we rented a car and drove to Niseko (more specifically, Hirafu). Hirafu itself is a ski village with lots of resorts for the ski season. However, in the off season, we were able to score some cheap rooms at the nicest hotel I've ever stayed at. Though it is known for their powdered snow in the winter, there was so much natural beauty in these smaller towns of Hokkaido. The drive from Hakodate was about 3 hours, and it was loaded with the most scenic drive I've ever taken. Rolling green hills, cozy farms and cottages, ocean views, mountains, valleys, rivers, and lakes. It felt like a Studio Ghibli movie. Anytime anyone asks me what my favorite part of the trip was, I always say just driving between towns.

That morning, we ate the amazing hotel breakfast and packed out bags to pick up our car at Toyota Rent-a-Car. Though a bit trippy at first driving on the "left" side of the road, after 30 minutes I was already a pro. I only drove in the wrong side of the road twice! If anything, the most annoying part was having the blinker stick on the right side of the steering wheel. In intense moments, I always accidentally hit the wipers which made things more hectic! Nonetheless, there wasn't too many drivers on the road in general so it was pretty easy.

I got a Toyota Yaris. It was like driving a Go-Kart, and the turning radius was insane

After shitting my pants for the first 30 minutes, I quickly got the hang of driving on the left. So much so that sometimes when I drive my car now, I think about how it feels awkward to drive on the right, and use the blinker on the left! Anyways, we took a detour up this viewpoint called Kijihiki Highlands Panorama Observation Deck. This is when the Studio Ghibli vibes start to kick in. We had views of Hakodate on one side, a view of these Lakes on another side, and then rolling green hills elsewhere. Again, no wind, no sounds of cars or people just pure nature and serenity.

This was our day to explore Lake Toya. It was about an hour drive away, and it might have the drive from Hakodate beat. Clear roads with views that looked like Windows wallpapers. There's a charm that I can't quite explain to the farmland here in Hokkaido, but I feel like it has something to do with the feeling of shibui. Unlike America, the land of the american dream, there were no fences saying "HEY! This is MY land, and that is YOUR land! See this fence? Don't touch MY land! If you do, I will SHOOT you!". But here, there is just a common courtesy of "I won't encroach on your land because it is your land and I respect you". Plus, there still felt like there was a respect for nature. Their land ended naturally with the curves of the earth, and if it wasn't needed, it was left untouched, given back to mother nature. I'm not sure how to cultivate these feelings, but if you do or would like to discuss, please help. Anyways, here's a photo of a pretty big farm we just pulled up next to.

As we got to Lake Toya, we navigated to this herbal cafe, and my god, this might have been the coolest place I've ever been. The photos couldn't encapsulate how serene life felt there. It felt like dining at your asian friend's house, and their asian mom feeds you and treats you with so much motherly love. If you ever go to Lake Toya, I highly recommend you stop here.

Since I was doing all the driving, and taking pictures in the car never come out good, this was one of my opportunities to capture what is called Pampas grass or Chinese silver grass. Only blooming in autumn, these were littered everywhere during our drives throughout Hokkaido, and they were my favorite. I feel like these feathery yellow ferns encapsulate the peacefulness Hokkaido has been giving us this whole time, which is something I've been learning to appreciate about life in my past couple of years. Something about the abundance of the grass feels one is embracing the overgrowth of nature and not trying to domesticate it, to see the beauty of change, and to enjoy fleeting moments. I feel like this is explored all over Japan, as where if things are too perfect, it gives one anxiety that one can mess it up, but to flow and embracing the everchanging state of the world, and anxiety disappears.

To me, these yellow ferns brought a bittersweet comfrot. To let them overgrow. To appreciate how mother nature affects the things we create. To appreciate the fleeting moments of the seasons. Quite opposite to the field of planted flowers in Furano/Biei (spoiler: we planned to but didn't end up going), or the fleets of Chinese tourists yearning for their souvenirs of clout through their cameras.

Finally, we reached Cape Kamui, and were greeted by fleets of tour buses filled with C****** t*******. Luckily, there was just leaving, so I only had to deal with them spitting in the urinals in the bathroom. To get to the view of the cape, you have to climb up this green hill.

At the top of the hill, you can see the hilly cape with a tiny lighthouse at the tip of it. The entrance of the pathway to the lighthouse was blocked off (not sure why), but the view of the empty trail was nice.

Although the view of the cape was amazing with the rocky shorts and the perfectly still blue ocean, I couldn't help but enjoy the back view of our viewpoint. Something about the endless layers of green and yellow hills sitting on top of the water.

We then headed to Otaru, and old port town. We perused Sakaimachi Hondori street, where we entered a bunch of glass shops and tricket stores, and tried a bunch of small street eats. We went to the LeTao flagship store and got a Madeline and a half cheese half milk soft serve. It was so good.

We then checked out the Otaru canal, which has now been remodeled as restaurants, cafes, and bars.

Otaru was a cute town, a bit touristy, but not as cool as we expected it to be. We ended our Otaru visit by going to the LeTao Pathos, which is their cafe. We got a grape cheesecake and this amazing souffle.

We then headed towards Sapporo. The drive was nothing special, just a 10 dollar tollway being the only road connecting the two cities. And then driving in Sapporo was NOT fun. Rainy, pedestrians at every crosswalk, and rush hour traffic. As we finally made it to our hotel, I went to return the car.
